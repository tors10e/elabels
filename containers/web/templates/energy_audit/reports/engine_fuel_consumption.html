Diesel engines are commonly used on Alaskan fishing vessels for propulsion, auxiliary AC, or hydraulic power generation. Brake specific fuel consumption (BSFC) is a measure of the efficiency of an engine. BSFC defines the amount of fuel required to produce a specified amount of work. All engines require some energy to overcome internal friction and compression forces. At light loads (less than 20%-30% of the engines rated HP) diesel engines have a higher BSFC (lowered efficiency) because the energy needed to overcome internal forces in the engine represents a higher percentage of the total load. As the load increases, BSFC is reduced and engine efficiency improves rapidly, reaching peak efficiency between 50 and 80% of the continuous load rating.

Simple steps to save fuel

“Right size” the engine for the anticipated load - Diesel engines are most efficient when running fully loaded and providing between 20 and 80% of their rated horsepower. At light loads, diesel engines use more fuel per horse power produced. Where possible, “right size” the engine for the average load.
Modern electronically governed diesel engines can be more fuel efficient, especially at light loads.
Minimize parasitic loads - de-clutch hydraulic pumps and deck hoses whenever possible.
Keep engine rooms well ventilated - For every 10° increase/decrease in engine room temperature, fuel consumption will increase/decrease by ~ 0.7%.
