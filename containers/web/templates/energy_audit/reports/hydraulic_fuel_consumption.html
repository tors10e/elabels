Hydraulic loads can be highly variable depending on the type of deck equipment used, the condition of the hydraulic pumps and motors, and the amount of heat the system generates when circulating hydraulic fluid. Hydraulically powered refrigeration systems are addressed in the refrigeration section. Field tests show that line loss (energy needed to circulate the oil through the lines ) can account for a significant fraction of the hydraulic load.

Simple steps to save fuel

Filter oil - Contaminant in hydraulic systems is widely recognized as the most frequent cause of hydraulic equipment wear and failure. Worn pumps and motors are less efficient and generate heat. Pre-season run the hydraulic system at the dock with a 2 micron fine filter installed. After a few hours, change the filter to a 10 micron water absorbing filter for the fishing season.
Minimize resistance - Keep lines short, avoid sharp corners, and minimize fittings. Replace worn pumps and motors. Heat and noise are signs of reduced efficiency.
Maintain proper oil viscosity - Operate ISO 32 oil around 100 degrees F and ISO 46 around 120 degrees F. Oil viscosity that is too low allows excessive fluids to slip past seals and can fail to provide adequate lubrication between components. Oil viscosity that is too high unnecessarily increases resistance to flow and may not reach between components with small clearances in order to lubricate them.
Turn off hydraulics when not in use - Disengage the hydraulic pump when not in use to minimize line loss.
